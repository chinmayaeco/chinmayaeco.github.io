Policy Constraints Under Fixed Exchange Rates

In a fixed exchange rate system, the central bank must maintain the exchange rate peg. This means:

  • Monetary policy is constrained - interest rates must align with the anchor currency
  • Fiscal policy becomes the primary tool for demand management
  • The central bank must use foreign reserves to defend the peg during pressure
  • Loss of reserves may force devaluation or austerity measures

Case Study: Maintaining a Fixed Exchange Rate

Scenario: Pressure on Currency Peg

Your economy is experiencing capital outflows due to:

  • Higher interest rates in the anchor currency country
  • Political uncertainty affecting investor confidence
  • Weakening export competitiveness
  • Growing current account deficit

Your Challenge: Defend the fixed exchange rate while minimizing economic disruption.

Policy Options Under Fixed Rates

With limited monetary autonomy, consider these responses:

  • Fiscal Contraction: Reduce government spending to lower imports and current account deficit
  • Reserve Intervention: Use foreign reserves to support the currency (temporary solution)
  • Capital Controls: Restrict capital outflows but risk long-term investment
  • Interest Rate Defense: Raise rates to attract capital (limited by economic impact)

Historical Example: Bretton Woods System

The Bretton Woods system (1944-1971) established fixed exchange rates pegged to the US dollar. Key lessons:

  • Successful while US maintained gold convertibility and price stability
  • Collapsed when US inflation undermined dollar credibility
  • Countries faced "impossible trinity" - cannot have fixed rates, free capital flows, and independent monetary policy simultaneously
  • Modern examples: China's managed rate, Gulf states' dollar pegs

Impossible Trinity Constraint

Under fixed exchange rates with open capital markets, monetary policy loses effectiveness. Businesses should:

  • Expect interest rates to follow anchor currency
  • Anticipate fiscal policy as main stabilization tool
  • Monitor foreign reserves for peg sustainability
  • Prepare contingency plans for potential regime change
